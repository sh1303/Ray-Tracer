Project Overview

This project implements a ray tracer from scratch in C++ as part of the CSCI 5607 ‚Äì Computer Graphics course. The goal was to build a fully functional ray tracing engine capable of simulating realistic lighting and material effects, including ambient, point, directional, and spot lighting, along with shadows, reflection, and refraction. The scenes are rendered using physically inspired parameters and exported as images to visualize the results.

Each test scene demonstrates a specific feature of the lighting or material models implemented. The results below include multiple lighting setups, material variations, and optical effects that show how rays interact with objects in the scene.

Difficulties Encountered

One of the main challenges I faced was debugging the geometry calculations‚Äîespecially ensuring that ray-sphere intersections were numerically stable and that reflected and refracted rays behaved correctly when bouncing off surfaces. Getting the camera‚Äôs aspect ratio right took several attempts because small mistakes in the projection math caused the spheres to appear stretched or flattened.

Another difficulty was handling the recursive nature of ray tracing. When I first added reflection and refraction, the image would either render completely black or blow out with overexposure due to uncontrolled recursion depth. Carefully limiting max_depth and balancing reflection coefficients finally produced smooth and realistic results.

I also struggled with balancing light intensity‚Äîspotlights were initially too bright and made scenes unrealistic. I tuned the falloff angles and experimented with both linear and cosine falloff functions until the lighting transitions looked natural. This process taught me how small parameter changes can drastically affect realism in rendered images.

Overall, this project helped me understand how low-level rendering pipelines work, and it gave me a deep appreciation for how much computation and fine-tuning go into producing a single realistic image.
